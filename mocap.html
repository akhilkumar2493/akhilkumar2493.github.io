Reliability of AI-Powered Markerless Motion Capture During Jump-Landing Tasks in Youth Female Athletes

Context

Markerless motion capture systems offer a faster, non-invasive alternative to traditional marker-based systems for biomechanical analysis. Before they can be adopted in clinical and field settings for ACL injury risk screening, their measurement reliability must be rigorously established, particularly in youth female athletes where ongoing maturation introduces unique variability into movement patterns.

What We Did

We recruited 30 youth female athletes across three maturity groups and captured three trials each of four jump-landing tasks using an eight-camera Theia3D system synchronised with Kistler force plates. We examined within-session relative reliability (ICC) and absolute reliability (SEM) at discrete time points and across the full landing phase time-series, with a subgroup analysis by maturity.

Aim 01

Does a markerless motion capture system (Theia3D) produce reliable measurements of lower-limb kinematics and kinetics during jump-landing tasks in youth female athletes?

Aim 02

How does biological maturation (pre-pubertal, pubertal, and post-pubertal) influence the reliability of these measurements across discrete time points and the full landing phase waveform?

Study Design

1
Participant Recruitment & Maturity Classification
Constraints
No prior maturity-specific reliability benchmarks Small group sizes limit statistical power Age ≠ maturation: biological classification needed
Informed by
Khamis & Roche (1994): PAH estimation Kraemer & Thiemann: reliability sample size ACL epidemiology literature
Process
30 athletes were randomly selected from a larger cross-sectional sample by a researcher external to the project. Standing and seated heights were measured to calculate percentage of predicted adult height (PAH). Participants were classified as pre-pubertal (PAH <86%), pubertal (PAH 86–95%), or post-pubertal (PAH >95%), with 10 athletes per group. Dominant limb was determined by asking which leg they would kick a ball with.
Why this approach?
Age-based grouping would have obscured the biological changes that actually drive variation in movement mechanics. PAH was chosen over other maturity markers (e.g. Tanner staging) because it is non-invasive, quick to administer in field settings, and has been validated for use in youth sport research. The minimum sample of 25 was determined by Kraemer & Thiemann's estimation for a desired ICC of 0.90 at 90% power. Three groups of 10 were used so that reliability could be compared across the maturation spectrum.
Fig 1. Participant stratification by biological maturity. 10 athletes per group (pre-pubertal, pubertal, post-pubertal) classified using percentage of predicted adult height (PAH) via the Khamis & Roche method.
2
Data Collection: Jump-Landing Tasks
Constraints
Markerless: no post-hoc marker correction possible Youth population: safety and trial rejection criteria critical Must capture ACL-relevant movement planes
Informed by
ACL injury mechanism literature Ford et al. (2007) Mok et al. (2016) DiCesare et al. (2015)
Process
Participants completed three successful trials each of four jump-landing tasks: a bilateral countermovement jump (CMJ), a drop vertical jump (DVJ) from a 30 cm box, a single-leg countermovement jump (SLCMJ), and a modified single-leg drop vertical jump (mSLDVJ). All tasks were performed with arms akimbo to eliminate upper limb compensation. Data were captured using an eight-camera Theia3D system operating at 100 Hz, synchronised with two Kistler force plates embedded in the floor at 1000 Hz. A standardised warm-up, visual demonstrations, and 3–4 practice trials were completed before data collection. Trials were rejected if hands left hips, if landing was unstable, or if the contralateral limb made ground contact during single-leg tasks.
Why four tasks?
A single task would not capture the variability in bilateral versus unilateral loading, or the difference between a self-initiated jump and a reactive drop landing. The DVJ in particular has been widely used in ACL injury risk screening due to its ability to expose frontal and transverse plane loading patterns. The SLCMJ and mSLDVJ were included because single-leg tasks place greater demands on neuromuscular control, where maturational differences in reliability were expected to be most pronounced.
3
Data Processing & Signal Filtering
Constraints
Cutoff frequency must be justified: not assumed Landing durations vary: normalisation required Proprietary Theia3D pipeline: limited transparency
Informed by
Winter (2009): residual analysis Theia3D software v2023.2 Visual3D (C-Motion)
Process
Video camera data were processed through Theia3D software to produce 3D kinematic data using deep neural network-based pose estimation and an inverse kinematic solution. A Woltring generalised cross-validity quintic-spline (GVCSPL) filter was applied to kinematic data at a 14 Hz cutoff frequency. Ground reaction force data were filtered using a fourth-order Butterworth zero-lag low-pass filter, also at 14 Hz. The landing phase of interest, from initial contact (vGRF >10 N) to the lowest vertical position of the centre of mass, was then normalised to 101 data points (0–100%) to enable pointwise time-series comparison across participants regardless of individual landing duration.
Why 14 Hz and 101 data points?
The 14 Hz cutoff was not a default assumption; it was determined through residual analysis (Winter, 2009), which plots the difference between filtered and unfiltered signals across a range of cutoff frequencies to identify the point at which signal is preserved and noise is removed. Normalisation to 101 points was chosen to standardise the time-series across the landing phase, enabling the pointwise ICC function to be computed at every percentage of the movement: a method that reveals which specific phase of the landing is most and least reliable, rather than collapsing everything to a single number.
Fig 2. Processing pipeline: raw video data from 8 cameras enters Theia3D (deep neural net pose estimation, inverse kinematics), filtered via Woltring GVCSPL at 14 Hz, exported to Visual3D, and normalised to 101 data points for time-series analysis.
4
Reliability Analysis: Discrete and Time-Series
Constraints
No existing time-series reliability data for Theia3D CMC underestimates reliability for small motions Subgroup sizes of 10 limit power: limb averaging used
Informed by
Pini et al. (2019): pointwise ICC framework Schelin et al. (2016): modified R code Koo & Li (2016): ICC interpretation Weir & Vincent (2005): SEM calculation
Process
Relative reliability was assessed using a two-way mixed, absolute agreement intraclass correlation coefficient (ICC₃,₁) at discrete time points, initial contact and peak instant, for 25 variables (16 kinematic, 9 kinetic). Absolute reliability was assessed via standard error of measurement (SEM) calculated from the mean square error of a repeated measures ANOVA. For the time-series analysis, a pointwise ICC and SEM were computed at each of the 101 normalised data points using a modified version of Schelin et al.'s (2016) R code. An integrated ICC, obtained by integrating the pointwise function across the full domain, provided a single global reliability index. The subgroup analysis averaged dominant and non-dominant limb values per participant per maturity group to improve statistical power given the n=10 per group.
Why pointwise ICC over the coefficient of multiple correlation?
The coefficient of multiple correlation (CMC), the most common waveform reliability metric in biomechanics, has documented limitations: it underestimates reliability for variables with small ranges of motion, it is insensitive to systematic bias across the waveform, and it ignores inter-individual variation. The pointwise ICC framework recommended by Pini et al. (2019) addresses all three limitations. It treats each time point as an independent observation, accounts for between-subject variance, and outputs a reliability profile across the entire movement, enabling researchers to identify exactly which phases of the landing are most susceptible to measurement error.
