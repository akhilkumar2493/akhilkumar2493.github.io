Influence of Biological Maturation on Jump-Landing Biomechanics and Limb Asymmetry in Youth Female Athletes

Context

Female athletes experience a significant increase in ACL injury risk during adolescence, driven by a neuromuscular lag in which rapid skeletal growth outpaces the development of muscle strength and motor control. This creates a window of vulnerability where dynamic landing mechanics deteriorate and inter-limb asymmetries emerge. Understanding precisely how biomechanical risk factors evolve across the maturation spectrum is essential for identifying when and where targeted injury prevention interventions should be applied.

Prior research has predominantly examined adult populations, focused on knee kinematics alone, and relied on discrete time-point measures that collapse the complexity of a dynamic movement into a single value. This study addressed all three gaps. Using a cross-sectional design, it aimed to determine how maturity status (pre-pubertal, pubertal, post-pubertal) influences lower-limb kinematics and kinetics at the hip, knee, and ankle during four jump-landing tasks, and whether kinematic and kinetic variables differ between the dominant and non-dominant limb across maturity groups.

Study Design

1
Participant Recruitment and Maturity Classification
Process
65 youth female athletes (ages 7–18) volunteered from multidirectional sports including football, basketball, volleyball, and netball. Eligibility required no lower-limb injury in the preceding six months and no history of ACL injury. Body mass, standing height, and parental heights were recorded to estimate maturity status using percentage of predicted adult height (PAH; Khamis and Roche, 1994). Participants were classified as pre-pubertal (PAH below 86%), pubertal (PAH 86–95%), or post-pubertal (PAH above 95%). Limb dominance was defined as the preferred leg for kicking a ball. Written parental consent and participant assent were obtained following full disclosure of procedures, benefits, and risks.
Why this approach?
Age is a poor proxy for biological maturation: two athletes of the same chronological age can be at vastly different developmental stages. PAH was chosen because it is non-invasive, quick to administer, and has been validated specifically for youth sport settings. Recruiting across multidirectional sports ensured the sample reflected the breadth of athletic populations most exposed to ACL injury risk.
Fig 1. Participant stratification by biological maturity. Pre-pubertal (n=14), pubertal (n=22), and post-pubertal (n=29) classified using percentage of predicted adult height (PAH) via the Khamis and Roche method.
2
Data Collection: Jump-Landing Tasks
Process
Participants completed four jump-landing tasks: a bilateral countermovement jump (CMJ), a drop vertical jump (DVJ) from a 30 cm box, a single-leg countermovement jump (SLCMJ), and a modified single-leg drop vertical jump (mSLDVJ). All tasks were performed with arms akimbo to eliminate upper-limb compensation. Whole-body kinematics were captured using an eight-camera Theia3D markerless motion capture system at 100 Hz, synchronised with Kistler force plates at 1000 Hz. A standardised warm-up and practice trials preceded data collection. Three successful trials per task were recorded for each participant.
Why four tasks?
Bilateral and unilateral tasks were included to capture a range of loading conditions. The DVJ has the strongest evidence base for exposing ACL-relevant frontal and transverse plane mechanics. Single-leg tasks isolate limb-specific loading and are particularly sensitive to neuromuscular asymmetries that emerge with maturation. Using both self-initiated and reactive drop-landing formats ensured the findings would generalise across sport-specific movement demands.
3
Data Processing and Biomechanical Modelling
Process
Motion capture data were processed in Theia3D to produce 3D joint kinematics via deep neural network-based pose estimation and an inverse kinematic solution. The landing phase was defined from initial contact (vGRF exceeding 10 N) to the instant of lowest vertical position of the centre of mass. Three-dimensional kinematics and kinetics were extracted at the hip, knee, and ankle across all three planes of motion: sagittal, frontal, and transverse. Net internal joint moments were derived via Newton-Euler inverse dynamics and referenced to the proximal segment. All computations were performed in Visual3D. Time-series data were normalised to 101 data points for waveform analysis.
Why include hip and ankle alongside the knee?
The majority of prior studies have focused exclusively on the knee, despite the hip and ankle playing central roles in force transmission along the kinetic chain. Aberrant hip adduction and internal rotation mechanics are established proximal contributors to knee valgus loading. Ankle mechanics influence ground contact strategy and distal load distribution. Restricting analysis to the knee alone would have missed the full picture of how maturation reshapes lower-limb movement strategy.
Fig 2. Biomechanical analysis framework: hip, knee, and ankle kinematics and kinetics extracted across the sagittal, frontal, and transverse planes for each jump-landing task.
4
Statistical Analysis: Discrete and Waveform Approaches
Process
A mixed model ANOVA examined the effects of maturity group (between-subjects) and limb dominance (within-subjects) on 25 discrete variables, including kinematic values at initial contact and peak instant and nine kinetic variables. Post-hoc tests with Bonferroni correction were applied where significant main effects were found. Effect sizes were reported as partial eta squared. For the waveform analysis, one-dimensional Statistical Parametric Mapping (SPM1D) was applied to time-normalised landing phase data using the spm1d library in MATLAB. A one-way ANOVA tested overall between-group differences, followed by pairwise post-hoc t-tests with Bonferroni correction to identify specific regions of difference across the landing phase waveform.
Why SPM alongside discrete analysis?
Discrete measures reduce a dynamic movement to a single time point, typically peak angle or moment. This approach misses rapid biomechanical transitions in the early landing phase and cannot show when during the movement differences between groups emerge or resolve. SPM preserves the full temporal structure of the data, treating the waveform as a continuous signal and identifying specific windows of the landing phase where maturity-related or limb-related differences are statistically significant. The combination of both approaches provided a more complete understanding than either method alone.

Lower panels show post-hoc pairwise comparisons between maturity groups (Group 1: pre-pubertal, Group 2: pubertal, Group 3: post-pubertal) with mean waveforms and 95% confidence bands.
