Accuracy of AI-Powered Motion Capture: A Validation Study

Markerless Motion Capture Validation · Cardiff Met · 2022–Present

"Can markerless motion capture reliably measure jump-landing biomechanics in youth female athletes?"
30
Participants
4
Jump Tasks
0.76–0.96
ICC Sagittal

Study Design

1
Participant Recruitment
MotivationMaturation-specific reliability data for markerless capture in youth female athletes — a population at disproportionate ACL injury risk — was absent from the literature.
Process30 athletes randomly selected from a larger sample and classified as pre-pubertal, pubertal, or post-pubertal using percentage of predicted adult height (PAH; Khamis & Roche, 1994). 10 per maturity group.
Why?Sample size determined by Kraemer & Thiemann — minimum of 25 for ICC of 0.90 at 90% power. Three groups of 10 enabled subgroup analysis; averaging limbs within each group improved statistical power.
2
Data Collection
MotivationCapture multi-planar lower-limb kinematics and kinetics during tasks that replicate real-world ACL injury mechanisms across bilateral and unilateral loading conditions.
Process3 trials each of CMJ, DVJ, SLCMJ, and mSLDVJ using 8-camera Theia3D at 100 Hz, synchronised with Kistler force plates at 1000 Hz. Arms akimbo throughout. Strict trial rejection criteria applied.
Why?Four tasks were chosen to cover bilateral and unilateral loading scenarios relevant to ACL screening. Markerless capture removes marker placement error and speeds collection — but its reliability must first be established before clinical adoption.
3
Data Processing
MotivationStandardise kinematics and kinetics for cross-participant and cross-task comparison, and enable time-series analysis across the full landing phase.
ProcessWoltring GVCSPL filter at 14 Hz applied following residual analysis. Landing phase (IC to lowest COM position) normalised to 101 data points in Visual3D.
Why?The 14 Hz cutoff was not assumed — it was determined through residual analysis (Winter, 2009). Normalising to 101 points enabled pointwise ICC comparison across participants regardless of individual landing duration.
4
Reliability Analysis
MotivationEvaluate whether Theia3D produces consistent measurements — at specific instants and across the full waveform — before it can be trusted for ACL risk screening.
ProcessICC₃,₁ (two-way mixed, absolute agreement) for relative reliability; SEM from RM-ANOVA for absolute reliability. Pointwise ICC and SEM calculated using a modified R script (Schelin et al., 2016) across all 101 normalised time points.
Why?Discrete time-point analysis alone misses variability across the waveform. Integrated pointwise ICC is more sensitive to systematic errors and between-subject variation than the coefficient of multiple correlation (CMC) used in older studies.
Tools: Theia3D · Visual3D · Kistler Force Plates · R · SPSS

Biomechanical Risk Modelling and Injury Prediction

Biomechanics & Injury Prevention · Cardiff Met · 2022–Present

"How do maturation-related changes in biomechanics drive ACL injury risk in youth female athletes?"
100+
Participants
2,400+
Papers Screened
2
Publications

Study Design

1
Systematic Review
MotivationIdentify which biomechanical risk factors for ACL injury had been established in the literature and where maturation-specific evidence was lacking.
ProcessSystematic search across PubMed, SPORTDiscus, and Web of Science — 2,400+ papers screened for evidence on maturation-related changes in lower-limb biomechanics during dynamic tasks.
Why?Before collecting primary data, a clear picture of existing evidence was needed to identify gaps, justify the study design, and select the most clinically relevant biomechanical variables to measure.
2
Participant Recruitment
MotivationBuild a sample that captures the full maturation spectrum — the window where biomechanical ACL injury risk changes most acutely in female athletes.
Process100+ youth female athletes from multidirectional sports (football, basketball, netball) recruited across maturation stages, classified by percentage of predicted adult height.
Why?Female athletes are 2–8× more likely to sustain ACL injuries than males, with risk peaking post-puberty. Stratifying by maturity — not age — captures the biomechanical changes that drive risk more precisely than age-based grouping.
3
Motion Capture & Biomechanical Modelling
MotivationQuantify how maturation alters knee loading mechanics during the movements most closely associated with non-contact ACL injury.
Process3D kinematics and kinetics collected during jump-landing tasks; joint angles and moments extracted across sagittal, frontal, and transverse planes. Regression modelling in R and SPSS to identify maturity-related predictors.
Why?Jump-landing tasks replicate the non-contact ACL injury mechanism. Multi-plane analysis was necessary because frontal and transverse plane loading — specifically knee abduction moment — are the strongest biomechanical predictors of ACL injury in the literature.
4
Screening Protocol Development
MotivationTranslate statistical findings into tools that coaches and clinicians can apply in practice to identify at-risk athletes.
ProcessBiomechanical risk thresholds derived from modelling results; findings validated against existing clinical screening tools and mapped to neuromuscular training programme design.
Why?Research impact in injury prevention depends on practical utility. Thresholds were deliberately designed to be interpretable by practitioners without biomechanics expertise, bridging the gap between lab findings and field application.
Tools: Motion Capture · Biomechanical Modelling · R · SPSS

Immersive Technology for Real-Time Performance Feedback

VR Kinematic Feedback System · Cardiff Met · 2022–Present

"Can real-time kinematic feedback delivered through VR improve athletic movement quality?"
30+
Participants
10%
Performance Gain
Meta Quest 3
Platform

Study Design

1
System Design & Prototype
MotivationAthletes traditionally receive movement feedback after completion — too late to correct errors within the same repetition. A real-time feedback system could change this.
ProcessEnd-to-end prototype developed on Meta Quest 3; real-time kinematic data pipeline built connecting Theia3D output to in-headset visual overlays during live dynamic tasks.
Why?Meta Quest 3 was selected for its standalone capability (no tethered PC), high-resolution display, and open development environment. Markerless capture via Theia3D was used to avoid restricting natural movement during tasks.
2
Feedback Delivery
MotivationDetermine the optimal form and timing of kinematic feedback to maximise its effect on movement correction without disrupting task performance.
ProcessLive kinematic data streamed from Theia3D and rendered as visual overlays within the headset. Feedback presented at key movement phases identified as critical for technique correction.
Why?Concurrent (real-time) feedback was chosen over terminal (post-movement) feedback because it allows athletes to adjust within the same trial, accelerating motor learning and reducing the lag between error and correction.
3
Testing Protocol
MotivationEvaluate whether real-time VR feedback produces measurable changes in movement quality compared to no-feedback conditions.
Process30+ participants performed standardised dynamic movement tasks under feedback and no-feedback conditions. Pre- and post-performance measures collected and analysed in MATLAB and R.
Why?A within-subject design was used to control for individual movement variability. Standardised tasks ensured performance measures were comparable across participants and conditions.
Tools: Meta Quest 3 · Theia · MATLAB · R

Wearable Sensor Analytics for Performance Prediction

GPS & Wearable Athlete Monitoring · Univ. of Los Lagos, Chile · 2021–2022

"Can wearable GPS sensors predict performance outcomes across a full elite football season?"

Elite Athletes
48 wks

Study Design

1
Sensor Deployment
MotivationCoaching decisions at Universidad de Los Lagos were largely intuition-based, with no objective longitudinal load data across a full competitive season.
ProcessMEMS-GPS sensors deployed across 20+ elite footballers for a 48-week competitive season; data collected across all training sessions and matches covering physical load, sprint velocity, cardiovascular intensity, and acceleration.
Why?A full 48-week season was necessary to capture within-season variation in load, fitness, and performance. Short-term studies miss the cumulative effects of training load on performance outcomes.
2
Data Processing Pipeline
MotivationRaw GPS data contains noise, drop-outs, and inconsistencies that must be resolved before any meaningful modelling can be performed.
Process400+ longitudinal athlete records cleaned and structured in MATLAB and R; outliers handled systematically; data organised by training type, match intensity, and individual maturity profile.
Why?Given the volume of data across 20+ athletes over 48 weeks, automated pipelines were built to ensure consistency and reproducibility. Manual processing at this scale would introduce unacceptable inconsistency.
3
Performance Modelling
MotivationIdentify which workload variables predicted performance outcomes and derive individualised thresholds that coaching staff could act on in practice.
ProcessRegression modelling in R to identify predictors of performance; workload and maturity status tested as independent variables; personalised thresholds derived for coaching application.
Why?Regression was appropriate given the continuous nature of performance outcomes and the need to quantify the relative contribution of each workload variable. Personalised thresholds — rather than group averages — were prioritised because individual variation in load tolerance is substantial in elite sport.
Tools: MEMS-GPS Sensors · MATLAB · R · Regression Modelling

Evidence-Based Optimisation of Human Performance

Background

Fast bowling speed is a critical performance determinant in cricket, yet the biomechanical and physical factors that predict it had never been systematically quantified using correlational evidence across the literature. Coaching decisions were largely based on intuition rather than data.

Aim

To identify biomechanical and physical determinants of fast bowling speed in cricket and translate findings into evidence-based strength and conditioning recommendations that coaches and practitioners can act on.

Methodology

A novel correlational meta-analytic framework was developed and applied to the cricket biomechanics literature. A systematic search was conducted across major databases. Correlational data was pooled and analysed in R and SPSS, and findings were mapped directly to strength and conditioning programming principles.

Results

Hip-shoulder separation and run-up velocity were identified as the strongest predictors of bowling speed. The novel framework was validated and subsequently adopted in related research. Published in Sports Biomechanics (2024) and International Journal of Sports Science & Coaching (2022).

Tools: R · SPSS · Correlational Meta-analytic Framework
