Accuracy of AI-Powered Motion Capture: A Validation Study

Markerless Motion Capture Validation · Cardiff Met · 2022–Present

30+

70%

1,500+
Trials Processed

Background

Traditional marker-based motion capture is the gold standard for measuring human movement, but it is time-intensive, lab-dependent, and requires significant post-processing. Markerless systems like Theia3D promise scalability for clinical and field use — but only if they can be shown to be accurate.

Aim

To determine the real-world accuracy and product readiness of a markerless motion capture system (Theia3D) across a range of dynamic movements, benchmarked against gold-standard Vicon marker-based capture.

Methodology

30+ participants performed a battery of dynamic movement tasks simultaneously captured by both Theia3D and Vicon. Key joint angles were extracted and agreement, reliability, and validity were assessed using R and MATLAB. Automated processing pipelines were built to handle large trial volumes efficiently.

Results

Strong agreement was found between Theia3D and Vicon across key joint angles during dynamic tasks. Automated pipelines reduced processing time by 70%, processing over 1,500 trials. The pipeline is now used as the standard lab workflow, and findings inform commercial product readiness assessment.

Tools: Theia3D · Vicon · MATLAB · R · Visual3D

Biomechanical Risk Modelling and Injury Prediction

Biomechanics & Injury Prevention · Cardiff Met · 2022–Present

2,400+

2

Background

ACL injuries are among the most serious in youth sport, with female athletes at significantly higher risk — particularly during puberty. Despite growing evidence, the movement patterns and maturation-related mechanisms that drive this risk are not well understood at a population level.

Aim

To identify knee injury risk patterns in youth female athletes using biomechanical modelling, and translate statistical findings into actionable screening and prevention strategies for coaches and clinicians.

Methodology

Large-scale quantitative studies with 100+ participants combined motion capture data with biomechanical modelling to quantify knee loading during dynamic movement tasks. Statistical analysis in R and SPSS. Findings were validated against clinical screening tools and mapped to prevention programme design.

Results

Distinct knee injury risk patterns were identified across maturation stages. Neuromuscular training was found to significantly reduce knee valgus and improve landing mechanics. Findings translated into evidence-based screening protocols and published in Sports Medicine (2024, 2025).

Tools: Motion Capture · Biomechanical Modelling · R · SPSS

Background

Athletes typically receive feedback about their movement after the fact — through video review or verbal coaching. Real-time feedback during the movement itself has the potential to accelerate skill acquisition and reduce injury risk, but applied VR tools in sport science remain limited.

Aim

To design and deploy a VR-based system using Meta Quest 3 that delivers real-time kinematic feedback during dynamic athletic tasks, and measure its effect on movement performance across participants.

Methodology

End-to-end prototype design and deployment using Meta Quest 3. Kinematic data was captured during movement and fed back in real time through VR overlays. Tested across 30+ participants on dynamic movement tasks, with pre- and post-performance measures collected and analysed in MATLAB and R.

Results

Real-time VR feedback produced a measurable 10% improvement in movement performance across participants. Findings support VR as a viable, scalable feedback tool in applied sport science. Manuscript currently in preparation.

Tools: Meta Quest 3 · Theia · MATLAB · R
Manuscript in Prep

Wearable Sensor Analytics for Performance Prediction

Background

Managing athlete workload across a long competitive season is critical for both performance and injury prevention. Wearable GPS and MEMS sensor data offers a high-resolution, individualised view of athlete load — but only if supported by robust pipelines and validated predictive models.

Aim

To deploy wearable sensors across a full elite football season, process longitudinal athlete data, and build personalised workload models that provide coaching staff with validated, actionable performance predictions.

Methodology

MEMS-GPS sensors were deployed across 20+ elite footballers for a 48-week competitive season. Over 400 longitudinal athlete records were cleaned and processed in MATLAB and R, covering physical load, cardiovascular intensity, sprint velocity, and acceleration. Regression modelling was used to identify key performance predictors.

Results

Workload and maturity status were found to explain 55% of performance variance. Personalised workload models improved training individualisation and recovery decisions. Findings published in Healthcare (2022) and Human Movement (2022).

Tools: MEMS-GPS Sensors · MATLAB · R · Regression Modelling

Evidence-Based Optimisation of Human Performance

Background

Fast bowling speed is a critical performance determinant in cricket, yet the biomechanical and physical factors that predict it had never been systematically quantified using correlational evidence across the literature. Coaching decisions were largely based on intuition rather than data.

Aim

To identify biomechanical and physical determinants of fast bowling speed in cricket and translate findings into evidence-based strength and conditioning recommendations that coaches and practitioners can act on.

Methodology

A novel correlational meta-analytic framework was developed and applied to the cricket biomechanics literature. A systematic search was conducted across major databases. Correlational data was pooled and analysed in R and SPSS, and findings were mapped directly to strength and conditioning programming principles.

Results

Hip-shoulder separation and run-up velocity were identified as the strongest predictors of bowling speed. The novel framework was validated and subsequently adopted in related research. Published in Sports Biomechanics (2024) and International Journal of Sports Science & Coaching (2022).

Tools: R · SPSS · Correlational Meta-analytic Framework
